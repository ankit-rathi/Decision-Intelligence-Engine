## **E. Correlation, Causation & False Confidence**

**Why:** Misinterpreting correlation as causation creates overconfidence and poor decisions.

**What:** Correlation is a **statistical association**; causation is a **mechanism linking action to outcome**.

**How:** Combine experiments, temporal analysis, and domain expertise to separate correlation from causation before acting.

---

From first principles, observing that two variables move together does not explain *why* they move together. Correlation simply means that when one variable changes, another tends to change in a predictable pattern. Causation requires a mechanism — a credible pathway through which one factor produces change in another.

The confusion arises because the human brain is wired to detect patterns and infer stories. If sales rise when advertising spend increases, it feels natural to conclude that advertising caused the growth. But both could be driven by a third factor, such as seasonal demand. Acting on correlation alone introduces false confidence — the illusion of control without validated leverage.

Consider a retail example. Data shows that customers who buy premium coffee machines also purchase gourmet beans. The correlation is strong. If a company assumes causation and aggressively discounts machines expecting bean sales to rise proportionally, it may misallocate margin. The true mechanism might be that affluent customers independently buy both products. Discounting the machine may attract price-sensitive buyers who do not purchase premium beans, breaking the assumed link.

Causation requires temporal order and mechanism. The cause must precede the effect, and there must be a plausible explanation connecting them. In credit risk, for example, late payments may correlate with default. But the causal mechanism could be underlying income instability. Intervening effectively requires addressing the mechanism, not merely reacting to the surface signal.

Experiments are the clearest tool for separating correlation from causation. Randomized A/B testing isolates the impact of a specific action by holding other variables constant. If Group A receives a new pricing strategy and Group B does not, differences in outcome can be attributed more confidently to the intervention. Temporal analysis also helps: if changes consistently follow specific actions, causal inference strengthens.

Domain expertise remains essential. Not all mechanisms are visible in data alone. Economic theory, behavioral insights, and operational knowledge help evaluate whether a relationship is plausible or spurious.

Misreading correlation as causation leads to fragile strategies built on unstable assumptions. Disciplined decision systems demand mechanism validation before scaling action. The objective is not finding patterns — it is identifying controllable levers that reliably change outcomes under uncertainty.
